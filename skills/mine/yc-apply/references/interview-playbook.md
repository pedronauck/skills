## The format

10-minute Zoom rapid-fire with 2-4 partners who have your application open during the call. Same-day decision; written feedback follows for interviewed applicants. All founders must be on the call. Treat 10 minutes as a floor, not a ceiling — excited partners sometimes run 14-15 min.

## The opening question

Always some form of "So what are you building / what is your company working on?" This is the ONE question to rehearse a single crisp sentence for, in plain English with concrete nouns. It lets partners anchor; a confused opener costs you the next 9 minutes.

## The 10-second / inverted-pyramid rule

Every answer: lead with the direct answer in one short sentence, then add proof/context. Partners interrupt freely; if you bury the lede they'll cut you before you reach it. Practice answering in ≤10 seconds, then elaborating only if asked.

## The three highest-signal facts

Before the interview, the founder picks the 2-3 strongest facts about the startup (best traction stat, strongest founder credential, sharpest unique insight) and ensures each lands at least once regardless of what's asked. 10 minutes flies by; maximize signal density.

## Mastery of your own numbers

Partners may grill micro-numbers and even Google-check live (the Lending Club valuation fact-check case). Have a one-page metric sheet ready: MRR, weekly/monthly growth rate, gross margin, take rate (marketplaces), cohort retention at 4/10/20 weeks (consumer), user count, paying-user count, CAC, LTV. The YC guide says have them written down to reference — you don't have to memorize, but you must not misstate.

## The "I don't like your idea, what else have you got?" probe

A Michael-Seibel/Emmett-Shear-style adversarial probe. Successful response: zero defensiveness, genuine curiosity ("why do you think it won't work?"), and a credible alternative idea on tap. Founders have been accepted for a DIFFERENT idea pitched on the spot (Athletes & AEs, Kopa, the S23 founder). The skill simulates this probe and scores the posture.

## Co-founder choreography

Jessica Livingston's green flags: mutual respect, collaboration. Red flags: one founder dominating, preventing the other from speaking, unequal equity. Enzo's rule: pre-divide questions by area (CEO → strategy/market; CTO → technical/team), no interruptions, no contradicting each other live.

## Demo readiness

Partners may ask to see the product live. Have it loaded in a browser tab, ready to screen-share in <10 seconds. Razorpay's live one-rupee payment is the canonical "demo won the interview" case. But: being able to explain in words still matters more than the demo — relying on a demo to explain the product is a bad sign.

## Mid-interview pivot is real

Partners bet on the founder, not the applied idea — the idea is a sample, not a contract. If waved off the idea, engage with the reasoning and pitch an alternative without defensiveness.

## The 5-question rapid-fire drill (Arjun format)

Run with a 10-second visible timer per answer:

1. What are you building?
2. What's your progress?
3. What's your unique insight?
4. How will you get your first 1000 customers?
5. Who are your competitors and why are you different?

After the drill, run the "I don't like your idea" probe. Then the metric-sheet check and the demo-readiness ping (24h before).

## Behavioral anti-patterns to flag during prep

- Memorized opener (>30 words, no number) → Shark-Tank voice → reject, rewrite.
- "Everything is great" with no candid challenge named → lack of self-awareness → force naming the single biggest current risk.
- Bullshitting instead of "I don't know / we haven't figured that out yet" → drill the honest answer.
- Flat, rehearsed energy → remind: walk in with genuine attention and a real smile (partners read earnest energy as positive). Do NOT over-coach personality.
- Any numeric / named-customer claim that isn't instantly defensible → fix before the interview (Dalton: misrepresentation = automatic disqualification).
