## The reader's model — 20 seconds per app, 100 apps per day

Paul Graham, `how-to-apply-to-y-combinator-y-combinator.md`: partners read independently, ~100 apps/day during the deadline window, ~20 seconds on the "what is your company going to make" question before deciding whether to keep reading.

Implications:
- Every answer's **first sentence carries 100% of the informational load**.
- Inverted-pyramid is not stylistic preference — it matches reader pacing.
- A confused or buzzword-filled opener is read as "founders cannot communicate clearly," which is a kill signal regardless of how good the rest is.

## The story-shape heuristic (Dalton)

Dalton Caldwell, `how-to-apply-and-succeed-at-yc-yc-startup-library-y-combinator.md`: after reading a strong application, the partner silently narrates the story:

> "This is the story of two friends. We're both programmers working on infrastructure at Facebook, and they quit their jobs three months ago to build a new cloud hosting company. They built a basic demo and have one first customer…"

If the partner cannot tell that story from the answers alone, the application failed. The five things being pulled out:

1. Who the team is.
2. What they're working on.
3. Why (how they got the idea).
4. What they've accomplished so far.
5. Why this will work.

The skill's Phase 6 gate runs a **story compile** check: write the Dalton-style story using only the drafted answers. Any blank that can't be filled → rewrite the relevant draft.

## Sam Altman's four evaluation questions

`2017-yc-annual-letter-y-combinator.md`:

1. Will this company build something **lots of people really love**? (with "lots" sufficiently large)
2. Will this company be **easy to copy**? (look for network effect, proprietary tech, complex coordination, or other moats)
3. Will these founders develop into **"forces of nature"**? (relentless determination, original thinking, communication, focus, intensity, finds-way-around-obstacles, "would I work for them?")
4. Does this company have a **clear and important mission**?

Plus a fifth filter: suspicion of "tracked resumes" — *"startups are not a resume item, and we don't like founders who view YC as a stop on the way to B-school."*

The skill runs all four as a self-grading gate in Phase 6.

## The founders question — most important on the form

Graham, same source: "Please tell us in one or two sentences about something impressive that each founder has built or achieved" is "the most important question on the application."

Rules:
- Pick the **single hardest intellectually difficult** thing each founder has done.
- Do not list the startup itself as the achievement.
- Adjectives are noise. Specific, verifiable accomplishments are signal.
- "It doesn't matter if it's not the sort of thing you'd put on a resume."

Anti-patterns the skill must flag:
- "Jordan is an exceptionally dedicated person who gives 100% effort." (no artifact)
- "Mary led product at FAANG." (role, not artifact)
- "We are passionate about..." (founder character claim with no evidence)

## Insight, not vision

Graham: founders prove they're smart by surfacing the **insight + the obstacles + a credible toehold plan**. He explicitly likes lines like *"we know that sounds impossible, but we think we can get a toehold initially by…"*.

> Even if the plan had only a 1% chance of working, it would be worth backing — because at least you have a plan.

Anti-pattern: claiming "well-designed and easy to use" is the moat. That is not an insight; it is just claiming you'll execute well. Every competitor claims the same thing.

## Disclosure beats concealment

Graham: *"Paradoxically, it is for this reason better to disclose all the flaws in your idea than to try to conceal them. If we think of a problem you don't mention, we'll assume it's because you haven't thought of it."*

Dalton echoes this for interviews: overconfidence = "denotes lack of self-awareness." Partners *want* candor about what is hard about the business.

The skill enforces a **flaws-disclosure worksheet** in Phase 4: the founder lists the top 3 strongest objections + their honest current answer (or "we don't know yet"). The skill then audits whether the application body addresses each.

## "Following directions" is itself a selection criterion

Dalton, in both 2018 and 2024 talks: *"This is one of the ways to tell how detail-oriented someone is — if they just follow directions. And so, pro tip: read all the stuff, follow the directions. We definitely weigh that in our decision."*

Sloppy applications (missing URLs, blank co-founder bios, missing video, 20-minute "spirit-journey" video instead of 1 minute) are read as evidence the team is not serious.

## The video matters — but quote the lift carefully

Do not claim "the video alone gives a ~5× lift." The sources support three *distinct* claims that must not be conflated:

1. **Video, qualitative:** Graham — *"Statistically we're much more likely to interview people who submit a video."* No multiplier attached.
2. **Combined criteria, ~4×:** Dalton — teams that meet the whole bar ("fills the whole thing out + good video + technical talent + evidence of seriousness") have roughly 4× higher interview odds. The multiplier is for the *combination*, not the video.
3. **Technical talent, ~5×:** Dalton's other talk — the ~5× figure is specifically about having technical talent on the team, not about the video.

So when advising a founder: the video is **not optional in practice** (claim 1), and the highest-leverage levers are a complete application + a builder on the team (claims 2–3). Never attribute a 5× lift to the video by itself.

## Repeat application is positive when progress is shown

Dalton: about a third (and per S22 batch announcement, ~50%) of every recent batch had applied before. Partners explicitly look for evidence the team:

- Addressed the previous interview feedback explicitly.
- Showed concrete progress between applications (revenue, users, hires, launches, retention).

Failing to engage with feedback (or arguing with it) is the worse path. The skill's reapplicant branch builds a delta-doc that maps each prior feedback point to a change or to evidence disproving it.

## Self-awareness beats confidence (interview signal)

Jessica Livingston, `tips-for-yc-interviews-y-combinator.md` + Dalton: founders who say *"Everything is great, everything is perfect. I have it all figured out"* score poorly. Acknowledge real challenges while still being confident. The partners are screening "Do I believe I can have a productive working relationship with these founders?" — because the partners on the call would later work with the team.

## The only documented "hack"

Sam Altman, `one-surprising-hack-to-get-into-yc-y-combinator.md`: *"If you've worked at a YC company, and get a good recommendation from the founder of that company, we'll give your YC application extra consideration."*

Caveat: this is the **only** documented exception to the "no networking" rule. Cold-emailing partners, sending physical objects, hiring "advisors" who claim YC connections = anti-patterns. The skill must surface the YC-portfolio recommendation channel separately from "no networking."

## What partners do NOT care about (defuse founder hesitation)

From `common-misconceptions-about-applying-to-yc-y-combinator.md` (S16 data) + `meet-the-yc-summer-2022-batch-y-combinator.md` (S22 data):

- Prior YC rejection: 45% of accepted founders had been rejected before.
- Not yet incorporated: 14.2%.
- No recommendation: 54.7%.
- Revenue or no revenue: ~50%/50% in S16; 71% had zero revenue in S22.
- Solo founder: 8.5%.
- Already raised money: allowed.
- Outside the US: 29% of S16 batch.
- Competitor already funded: YC explicitly funds parallel companies in the same space.

The skill defuses these hesitations in Phase 0 and again in Phase 6.
