## The Seven Pitch Questions (Michael Seibel)

The founder must answer all seven in ≤2 sentences each before drafting any application text. If any answer is >3 sentences or contains "platform," loop until tightened.

1. **What do you do?** Lead with name + concrete user path. "Predigested … like baby food." Concrete nouns only.
2. **How big is the market?** Prefer bottom-up over top-down. ("N customers × $X they pay × Y% conversion = $Z" beats "$140B TAM × 1%".)
3. **What's your progress?** Ratio of work to time elapsed. Product + customers > biz dev or fundraising.
4. **What's your unique insight?** "Derived from multiple conversations with customers, deep analysis of current products in the space, and often personal experience." NOT "passion."
5. **What's your business model?** "Own the simple business model." One clear model, not "ads + subscriptions + licensing + marketplace."
6. **Who's on your team?** Number of founders, technical co-founder yes/no, how long they've known each other, full-time yes/no, equity split — *hopefully equal*.
7. **What do you want?** State the ask plainly. No dancing around it.

## The Email Test (mandatory gate)

Seibel: write a two-sentence pitch, email it to a smart friend, ask them to explain it back.

> "If they ask any clarifying questions, you need to revise your pitch."

Email (not a live conversation) is mandatory because you cannot add clarifications mid-stream the way you can in person — it mirrors the way a YC partner reads the application cold.

Skill enforcement: in Phase 2, draft the two-sentence pitch, require the founder to send it and paste back the friend's paraphrase. If the paraphrase diverges from intent, the pitch is not clear yet — loop.

## Idea Quality Score (Jared Friedman, 4 criteria, averaged)

Score 1-10 on each; average them. If average <6 or any single dimension <4, the founder must strengthen that dimension or write a paragraph naming the weakness honestly.

1. **How big could it be?** Look at existing large companies in/near the space, OR a small market with a credible path to enormous.
2. **Founder–market fit.** (Flexport's Ryan, 10 years in international trade = 10/10. A generic founder with no domain contact = low.)
3. **Confidence the problem is big.** Ideally from personal experience.
4. **A new, important insight.** (Airbnb founders had personally invited strangers via Craigslist and discovered the danger model was wrong.)

Founder-market fit can be **earned**, not just born: Junestrand interviewed 100 lawyers over lunch (`how-this-25-year-old-built-a-675m-legal-ai-startup…`); Rick Morrison spent $15-20k of personal money on conferences before founding (`comprehend-s-rick-morrison…`). The skill accepts earned fit if the founder documents the work (conversations conducted, hours in the field, money spent learning).

## Four idea-generation mistakes (flag in founder narrative)

From `how-to-get-startup-ideas…`:

1. **Waiting for a brilliant idea.** Google was the 20th search engine; Facebook the 20th social network.
2. **Leaping on the first idea.** "You'll spend years on this — spend two weeks deciding."
3. **Starting with a solution, not a problem.** "Uber for plumbers" = SISP (solution in search of a problem).
4. **Believing ideas are scarce.**

## Four idea filters that unconsciously reject great ideas

Counterintuitively, the things that make founders hesitate are often positive signals:

1. **"Seems hard to start"** (Stripe).
2. **"Boring space"** (Gusto).
3. **"Too ambitious"** (Boom).
4. **"Existing competitors"** (Dropbox). Existing competitors = demand exists. **No competitors usually means no market.**

Skill use: ask "Which of these four filters would have caused you to NOT start this?" Use the answer to phrase the application's "why this is non-obvious" framing.

## Seven idea recipes, ranked by hit rate

Best → worst:

1. **Things your team is uniquely good at** (SnapDocs, Mixpanel, Lattice — half of YC's most successful companies trace here). **Best.**
2. Things you personally wish existed.
3. Things that just became possible (new tech, new regulation).
4. New variants of successful companies (use with caution).
5. Underexploited big trends.
6. Crowdsourced from talking to people.
7. **"Uber for X" / "Airbnb for Y"** — tends to be SISP. **Worst.**

Skill use: have the founder declare which recipe their idea came from and own the weakness of weaker recipes. If recipe is #4, #6, or #7, require an extra "why this isn't a SISP" answer.

## "Make it sound dumber" — clarity rule

Kevin Hale's three rules for any compressed-information format (slides, written answers, video): **legible, simple, obvious.** Seibel's version: "make it sound dumber than you think it should."

Critical nuance: **predigested ≠ undersold.** The target is "complex idea, simple language," NOT "simple idea, simple language." A frontier-AI or biotech founder must still demonstrate technical fluency — just in plain words.
