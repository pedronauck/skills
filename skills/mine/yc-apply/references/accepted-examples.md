## 50-character description examples

Hard cap: 50 characters. No buzzwords. Concrete noun + what it does.

- **[FOUNDER-REPORT] Anja Health (W22):** "Preserving stem cells at birth for preventative care." (`reading-my-accepted-y-combinator-application…`)
- **[TRANSCRIPT] Hera:** "AI motion designer." (`how-you-can-get-into-y-combinator-in-2026-…-2.md`) — 19 chars; brutally short works.
- **[TRANSCRIPT] Static EV (S20):** "Smart public EV charging infrastructure." (`how-you-can-get-into-y-combinator-in-2026-…1.md`)

The skill drafts ≥5 candidate descriptions, validates each ≤50 chars, scans for buzzwords, then grills the founder to the sharpest one.

## "What is your company going to make?" — 3-sentence template

Pattern (from Hera's accepted answer): **(1) factual product description with concrete nouns → (2) the current alternative and its specific shortcoming → (3) who uses it / one concrete proof point.**

> **Hera:** "Hera makes professional motion graphics accessible. The only alternative today, Adobe After Effects, is complex and time-consuming. Hera lets anyone produce professional-quality visuals in seconds with our template marketplace that grows with each user and a design agent that ensures good design."

**[OFFICIAL]** Other exemplars partners cite directly: GitLab's application ("Over 100,000 organizations use it… thousands of programmers at Apple"), Dropbox's S07 application (linked from Graham's essay).

## Founder bio / video examples

**Embark Trucks / Varden Labs (W16) — full accepted video.** Structure: three intros, each ending on a credential anchor, plus an explicit "how long have you known each other" answer.

> "Hi I'm Alex, this is Brandon and Mike… I've been programming since I was 13, ranked top-20 programmer in Canada in high school, most recently a software engineering intern at Khan Academy in Mountain View. … I'm Mike, I do mechanical — built my own fully custom 3D printer, electric scooter, and worked at YC alum Thalmic Labs as mechanical intern. … I'm Brandon, software + electrical, worked at three startups, most recently an AI company programming robots. … The three of us were in the same mechatronics engineering class at Waterloo, we've lived together for two years and worked on lots of projects together."

Lesson: each founder anchors on a **built artifact or a recognizable prior employer**, never an adjective.

## Traction / "how far along are you?" examples

- **Hera:** "60 paying users who are part of their creative workflow. 35k organic signups, 5K MRR."
- **Anja (video):** "We've already made a couple of sales, have a growing waitlist of 2,000 parents, a TikTok platform of ~20,000 followers, and just made our first hire."

Lesson: exact numbers, defined terms (paying vs signups vs followers), no "some" / "a few."

## "How will you make money?" examples

> **Anja:** "DTC e-commerce with 40% margins. Customers can pay upfront or opt into our subscription plan. 30% of Americans make $50K+, enough to afford at least our $35/month payment plan. Total payments range $3K–$7K per customer."

Lesson: name the model (one model), the margin, the price, and a bottom-up sizing of who can afford it.

## "Who are your competitors? What do you understand that they don't?" examples

> **Anja:** "Generate Life Sciences leads the industry but neglects their low NPS scores, branding, social media, contemporary avenues like doula groups, and the 97% of parents who have never heard of cord blood banking."

> **Anja (interview):** "People are already doing what we do, but the incumbents are archaic. They don't do placenta banking, they don't prioritize accessibility, and they have no community or social-media presence or proprietary stem-cell processing."

Lesson: name the real incumbent, then state the specific gap you exploit. Never "we have no competitors."

## "What do you know that others don't?" / founder-market fit examples

> **Anja (interview):** "I personally know how to reach parents where they are. I've demonstrated this by building a TikTok following of 167,000, and we see comments every day like 'I gave birth 4 days ago and nobody told me about this.'"

> **Anja, why-this-idea:** "I was inspired because of my brother Andrew, who had cerebral palsy. My parents looked into umbilical cord blood as a treatment…"

Lesson: anchor "why you" in a concrete biographical fact + a demonstrated unfair advantage.

## Reapplication "what changed" examples

> **Static EV (W20 → S20, accepted 2nd try):** "Last time we didn't have a fully developed product but now we do. We've gained two B2B customers, we have people using the app, we got incubated by NASSCOM, got office space, and won a competition."

Honest-weakness example (also Static EV): asked "who do you fear most?", the founder left the field empty and explained — "the industry was nascent, these competitors weren't doing this anyway, so I was honestly not fearful." Refusing to invent a fear read as honest, not evasive.

## Interview opener examples (deliberate-hook pattern)

> **Hera:** "We're building Hera, an AI motion designer. You can think of it as Lovable for animated videos. We just launched one week ago and we got 60 paying users."

Lesson: one-sentence plain answer + one traction stat engineered to trigger the partner's follow-up. The 10-second-rule: lead with the answer, then proof.

## How the skill uses this file

For each high-stakes field in Phase 4: surface the 2-3 matching examples above, then ask the founder to draft. The examples calibrate the bar; the founder's answer must be their own. Never paste an example into the founder's application.
